  • Why media research for India?

    • The need

      • In November 2017, Nic Dawes gave a keynote at Media Indaba. He spoke about how every 12-18 months, there is a new wave in the news industry that generates a lot of enthusiasm but the resulting activity does not result in progress.
      • There is a need to study the phenomenon at a more fundamental level. There is a need for a body of work that synthesizes the units as a whole.
      • The two major uncertainties — posing an existential threat to media organizations — are the collapse of business models and the second is the rise of misinformation. While most of the industry continues to experience the repercussions of these phenomena, there is little to no clarity on the whys and hows.

    • Media research in India

      • India does not have a strong civic media research practice of its own. The majority of organizations observing media from outside - with the likes of TAM, BARC, Media Research Users Council (MRUC), Neilson, Kantar, Eldman India, etc. work on themes such as audience measurement, ad-spending of advertisers and market research.
      • Even if other think tanks do media research, it is a secondary intersection with their core focus, e.g., legal, international relations, etc.
      • We found that research in civic media happens at organizations like Center for Media Studies, Center for Internet and Societies, and Digital Empowerment Foundation.
        • Center for Media Studies was founded in 1991 as India's first Media Lab to study trends in electronic and print media and develop strategies.
        • Centre for Internet and Societies (CIS)'s News&Media division is a regular partner with the Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism (RISJ).
        • Digital Empowerment Foundation mainly works on digital media literacy through its Research and Advocacy program. Currently, they are working on the themes of Online Content, Misinformation, "Fighting Fake News" and "Tracking the troll."

  • What do we investigate?

    • PROTOSignals attempts to overcome this gap, connecting academic studies as well as tactical insights that businesses have acquired over the years by studying the ecosystem ground up, and assessing the very fundamentals on top of which Indian journalism is practiced.
    • We want to pick research questions in The Business of News, entrepreneurial journalism, and Information Disorder. Our effort is to answer questions that result in tangible insights and develop understanding that can have practical applications.

  • What are we not?

    • We are not a media watchdog and do not pick up work that names and blames people or companies. We also do not pick up work that focuses on issues of a religious, cultural, social or political nature.

  • Who is working at PROTOSignals?

    • The problems faced by news media are nuanced, and professionals from any one background could have a blindspot. Hence, we have built an interdisciplinary team of technologists, entrepreneurs, journalists, and academicians.

  • How do we investigate?

    • The goal is to bring ideas, frameworks, and research techniques from different industries and practices to study the challenges faced by civic media. Here is one of the methods we follow: Deconstruction

    • We aim to pick up work that meets following criteria:

      • Each project should add to the global body of knowledge by adding the Indian context. Alternatively, we pursue research questions that can be best tested in India and build novel research there.
      • Even though the larger line of inquiry lasts years, we should be able to ship something every 3-6 months. Each such output is complete in itself that it looks at the problems from one context yet incomplete because much more needs to be dug.
      • We have a good understanding of technology, and data science. Hence, most of our research might be empirical and can have technology demonstrators.

  • Who is funding our work?

    • We work with organizations and institutions who are invested in the future of digital - technologies, tools, data and media - as a driver of impact around specific civic issues.
    • Our funders also realize that most of the problems that PROTO is working on are nuanced and there is no direct answer. Hence, research plays a critical role in discovering new ideas.

  • What is the interplay between our research and community work?

    • They are complementary

      • Both activities are complementary to each other. Research provides novel information to our community efforts (webinars, meetups, training, blogs). The community offers research teams access to the ecosystem for interviews. These are mutually beneficial and inseparable activities.

    • Yet different

      • The community team builds a platform to give voice to the media owners, innovators, and experts (primary audience). Hence, it is an inch-deep, mile-broad effort. They need to have a strong brand presence, and each published output (webinar, blog post, etc.) should be finished and refined. Finally, each effort takes a few weeks to produce. The material they produce is essentially a digest of all other work done out there.
      • The research team needs to find novel ideas to investigate and seek visibility/validation by publishing platforms where other researchers, academia (primary audience) publish. It is an inch-broad, mile-deep effort. Each output takes 4-6 months with some projects taking longer. Until it is ready, we need to build-in-public, i.e., blog about work-in-progress concepts so we can have better conversations with other researchers.
